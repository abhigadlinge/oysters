data science training using excel

For The Faster, Accurate, and Reliable Data science training

get a quote

Microsoft Excel is probably the most well-known tool for working with data. Almost everyone knows it, we have our own hate or love relationship to tables, pivots, VBA-scripts and charts. We do our monthly business spreadsheets, calculate vacation costs and even work with contact lists in Excel. The word “Excel” has even become a synonym to “table” or set of tables, as in: “hey, can I send you an Excel?” Even your grandmother has used it. After all, it has been around since 1987 and still looks fairly similar with its grids and sheets. They probably got something right in that design?

But wait. Data Science? In Excel?? Isn’t data science about hyped Jupyter Notebooks in the web browser, Big Data and Hadoop in the Cloud, Deep Learning on the GPU and advanced interactive plots and visualizations, and …? This may sound far from Excel as you know it. Well, hang on…

There are probably plenty of arguments for this (and against). But before starting, let’s just clarify: I don’t have any professional relationship with Microsoft and never have had.

Best editor for 2D data

There’s simply no better editor for 2-dimensional data (i.e. tables). Probably that’s why Excel has had the same layout over the years. The tables are easily edited, formatted, colourized, and shared. Google Sheets is a clear confirmation of the Excel design for editing data — but scaled for multiple users. Allowing multiple users to edit simultaneously is something I miss in Excel (after losing data on SharePoint and OneDrive too many times). The ease of just sharing a link is probably also part of the success behind data science notebooks such as Jupyter.

In Excel, however, one keypress [F11] turns your table into a chart using your company’s visual identity (given that someone took the time to set up the Theme). Add to that the built-in tables, filters, slicers, groupings, window splitting, cell formulas and other features and any alternative table or database editor stands flat. That is, as long as you are fine with a maximum of 1,048,576 rows and 16,384 columns per sheet! Not very impressive for Big Data, but for editing and looking at data in practice, this is often enough.

Scripting Excel — like a Data Scientist

You would, of course, like to use your favourite data science scripting language (Python, R, Scala, Julia, Java, JavaScript, …) and trusted libraries. That is just fine: you can use Python with Excel. At least if you’re on a Windows machine (which I recommend, as Excel on Mac still isn’t very stable).
